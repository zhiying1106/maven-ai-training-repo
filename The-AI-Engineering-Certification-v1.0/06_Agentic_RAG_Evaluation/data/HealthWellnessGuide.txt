# Health & Wellness Guide: Course Evaluation Corpus

This short course corpus is for learning retrieval and evaluation. It offers
general wellness information, not diagnosis, treatment, or individualized
medical, nutrition, or mental-health advice. A reader with persistent,
concerning, or worsening symptoms should consult a qualified health
professional. Seek urgent help for emergencies.

## Movement: build a routine gradually

For many adults, the public-health target is at least 150 minutes of
moderate-intensity aerobic activity each week, or 75 minutes of
vigorous-intensity activity, plus muscle-strengthening activity on two or more
days each week. The time can be spread across the week and broken into smaller
sessions. Some activity is generally better than none, and a gradual increase
can make a new routine more manageable.

Examples of moderate activity can include brisk walking, cycling, swimming, or
dancing, depending on a person's health, ability, and setting. Muscle
strengthening can include resistance bands, weights, body-weight movements, or
some yoga postures. Older adults may benefit from including balance activities.
People with chronic conditions, disabilities, recent injuries, or concerns
about a new activity plan should ask a health professional what is appropriate
for them.

## Movement: daily practicality

Choose movement that fits the day: walking part of an errand, taking stairs when
appropriate, short movement breaks, or scheduling an activity with another
person. Consistency is more useful than an all-or-nothing plan. Tracking a
weekly total and noting how an activity feels can help someone adjust a routine
gradually. Pain that is severe, new, worsening, or paired with weakness,
numbness, fever, injury, chest symptoms, or other concerning signs needs
professional assessment rather than a generic exercise plan.

## Sleep: routine and environment

Sleep supports health, mood, attention, and daily functioning. CDC guidance says
adults ages 18 to 60 generally need seven or more hours of sleep per night;
needs vary by age and person. Practical habits that can support sleep include a
consistent sleep and wake time, a quiet and comfortable bedroom, regular daytime
activity, and reducing exposure to electronic devices close to bedtime. Avoiding
large meals, alcohol, and late-day caffeine may also support sleep for some
people.

If someone regularly has trouble falling asleep, wakes repeatedly, remains tired
despite enough time in bed, or is concerned about a possible sleep disorder,
they should discuss it with a health professional. A simple sleep diary—bedtime,
wake time, naps, caffeine, activity, and how rested the person feels—can make
that discussion more concrete.

## Stress and recovery

Stress-management practices are personal and work best when they are realistic
enough to repeat. A short walk, a brief breathing pause, a transition ritual at
the end of work, writing down the next task, speaking with a trusted person, or
protecting time for rest can all be small recovery practices. The goal is not to
eliminate every stressor; it is to notice patterns and create a few reliable
ways to recover.

Persistent distress, inability to manage daily life, thoughts of self-harm, or
an immediate safety concern require support beyond a wellness routine. In an
immediate emergency, contact local emergency services or an appropriate local
crisis resource.

## Food and hydration: use flexible patterns

Instead of treating a single food as a cure, build meals around a varied pattern
of foods that fits cultural preferences, access, health needs, and budget.
Regular meals and access to water can support daily energy and concentration.
Nutrition needs can change with medication, pregnancy, kidney disease,
diabetes, allergies, eating disorders, or other health conditions, so
individualized questions belong with a qualified clinician or registered
dietitian.

## Planning a realistic week

A practical weekly plan can connect movement, sleep, and recovery. For example,
someone might schedule short walks on several days, reserve two days for
strength-focused activity, choose a consistent wind-down time, and review at the
end of the week what felt sustainable. The plan should be adapted to the
person's circumstances rather than treated as a prescription. A missed day is
information for planning, not a reason to abandon the whole routine.
